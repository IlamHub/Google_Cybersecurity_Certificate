# Course 8: Put It to Work — Prepare for Cybersecurity Jobs

## Course Overview

This final course bridges the gap between technical skills and professional readiness. It covers the mindset and behaviors expected of a working security analyst — including how to detect and escalate incidents, communicate with stakeholders, stay current in the field, and prepare for the job search and technical interviews.

---

## Module 1: Protect Data and Privacy in the Workplace

**Focus:** Security mindset, data classification, asset protection, and business continuity.

### Topics Covered

- **Security as a mindset** — approaching every task with the awareness that decisions have security consequences.
- **Data and asset classification** — understanding how data sensitivity levels (public, internal, confidential, restricted) determine the controls applied to protect them.
- **Asset protection** — mapping assets to appropriate safeguards based on classification.
- **Disaster recovery and business continuity** — distinguishing between disaster recovery plans (restoring systems after an incident) and business continuity plans (maintaining operations during disruptions).
- **Juliana's story: Asset protection** — a real-world scenario illustrating how analysts make asset protection decisions under pressure.

## Module 2: Escalate Incidents

**Focus:** Understanding when, why, and how to escalate security incidents — and the professional impact of those decisions.

### Topics Covered

- **The importance of escalation** — recognizing that analysts are not expected to resolve every incident alone; escalation protects the organization and the analyst.
- **Escalate with a purpose** — escalation should be intentional, timely, and directed to the right person or team.
- **Recognize roles and responsibilities during escalation** — understanding who handles what during an incident (SOC analyst → incident responder → CISO chain).
- **From simple activity to major data breach** — how seemingly minor events (a single failed login, a misconfigured firewall rule) can escalate into significant incidents if not caught early.
- **When and how to escalate** — practical guidance on escalation triggers, communication style, and documentation.
- **Escalation timing** — acting too slowly risks damage; acting too hastily can disrupt operations. Timing requires situational judgment.
- **Juliana's story: Attention to detail** — a scenario demonstrating how careful observation and timely escalation prevented a larger incident.

## Module 3: Communicate Effectively to Influence Stakeholders

**Focus:** Identifying stakeholders, tailoring communication to their needs, and using visual tools to convey security information clearly.

### Topics Covered

- **Stakeholders in cybersecurity** — stakeholders include executives (CISO, CTO), legal and compliance teams, IT managers, and business unit owners. Each has different priorities and different levels of technical knowledge.
- **The purpose and impact of stakeholders** — stakeholders make decisions that affect security budgets, policies, and response priorities. Clear communication shapes those decisions.
- **Clear and concise communication** — security reports should avoid unnecessary jargon when writing for non-technical audiences. Precision and brevity improve uptake.
- **Building blocks of cybersecurity communications** — structure, context, impact, and recommended action are the four elements of an effective security message.
- **Communicate effectively with stakeholders** — matching tone, depth, and format to the audience (a one-page executive summary vs. a full technical incident report).
- **Visual storytelling in cybersecurity** — dashboards, charts, and timelines allow stakeholders to quickly grasp trends, severity, and scope without reading dense reports.
- **Create visual dashboards for impactful cybersecurity communications** — using tools like Google Slides, Looker, or SIEM dashboards to present security metrics visually.
- **Juliana's story: Effective communication** — a scenario showing how proactively communicating risk data to a manager helped justify a security investment.

## Module 4: Engage with the Cybersecurity Community

**Focus:** Staying current with evolving threats and building a professional network in the cybersecurity field.

### Topics Covered

- **Helpful cybersecurity resources** — NIST, CISA, OWASP, CVE databases, vendor security bulletins, and threat intelligence feeds as go-to sources for staying informed.
- **Strategies for engaging with the cybersecurity community** — following security researchers on social platforms, reading blogs, participating in CTF (Capture the Flag) competitions, and attending conferences.
- **Engage with the cybersecurity community in a meaningful way** — contributing to forums (Reddit r/netsec, Stack Exchange), sharing threat intelligence, and participating in open-source security projects.
- **Connect with other cybersecurity professionals** — LinkedIn, local ISAC chapters, DEF CON, Black Hat, and BSides events as networking opportunities.
- **Victoria's and Sarah's stories** — perspectives from working professionals on how continuing education and community involvement accelerated their careers.

## Module 5: Find and Apply for Cybersecurity Jobs

**Focus:** AI tools in cybersecurity, technical interview preparation, and completing the certificate program.

### AI in Cybersecurity

- **Introduction to AI in Cybersecurity** — how generative AI is changing the speed and scale of both attacks and defenses.
- **Use generative AI to work smarter and faster** — practical applications: drafting incident reports, summarizing logs, generating regex patterns, and explaining complex frameworks.
- **Decode complex security frameworks with AI** — using AI to break down NIST, CIS Controls, MITRE ATT&CK, and SOC 2 requirements into plain language.
- **Use AI to help identify bugs** — prompting AI tools to assist with code review and vulnerability identification.
- **Refine code with help from AI** — using AI for iterative code improvement, such as optimizing Python scripts used in security automation.
- **Understand system vulnerabilities with AI** — AI-assisted threat modeling and attack surface analysis.
- **Prioritize alerts with AI** — using AI to triage high volumes of SIEM alerts and reduce alert fatigue.
